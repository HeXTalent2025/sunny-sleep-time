Lily and the Pink Shell

A story set at Mooloolaba Beach
🐚
Lily loved Mooloolaba Beach more than anywhere in the whole wide world.

She loved the way the waves came rushing in — whooooosh — and then went rushing back out again. She loved the smell of the salty air and the way the sand squished between her toes.

She loved the big seagulls that walked along the water's edge, and the little crabs that scuttled away under the rocks.

"Ready?" said Mum.
"Ready!" said Lily.

And off she ran.
✦
Lily ran right up to the water and let the little waves chase her feet.
Cold! Bubbly! Fun!

She splashed and squealed and splashed some more. Then she stopped.

There — right there in the wet sand — something small and shiny and pink.

Lily crouched down and picked it up very carefully. It was a shell. Round and smooth and perfect, with little pink and cream stripes all the way around.

The prettiest shell Lily had ever seen.

"Hello, shell," she whispered.
✦
Lily held the shell up close to her ear, the way Dad had showed her once.

Shhhhh.

And there it was — the sound of the ocean, right inside the shell. A tiny little whooooosh, just for her.

She tucked it safely into her pocket and patted it twice.

All the way home, she could feel it there — her special Mooloolaba treasure. And that night, when she held it up to her ear again in bed, the waves were still whispering.

They always would be.
Age 5

Oscar and the Currawong

A story set in Noosa National Park
🦅
Oscar pulled on his hat and buckled his sandals twice — because once wasn't enough for a Noosa day.

The drive took twenty-two minutes. Oscar had been counting since he was four, when Dad told him it was exactly twenty-two minutes from home to the national park. It was always exactly twenty-two.

When the doors opened, the air smelled different here. Green and cool and alive with sounds he couldn't quite name yet. He jumped out before Mum had even turned off the engine.

"Shoes, Oscar."

He put them back on.
✦
The track wound through the trees, dappled and cool, the ocean flickering blue between the branches whenever the path curved just right.

Oscar ran ahead, then stopped, then ran again. There was always something to look at — a lizard on a warm rock, a spider web catching the light, the tiny tracks of something small pressed into the mud.

"Slow down," Mum called. "You'll miss everything."

Oscar wasn't sure how you could miss something by running toward it. But he waited anyway, hopping from foot to foot, while she caught up.
✦
That's when he heard it — a long, wobbly call that seemed to rise and fall and change its mind halfway through, echoing off the gum trees like it was searching for something.

Oscar froze.

There, on a branch just above the path, sat the biggest black bird he had ever seen. Glossy dark feathers. Bright yellow eye. Head tilted to one side as if it was thinking something important.

A currawong.

It looked at Oscar. Oscar looked at it. Neither of them moved.

Oscar held his breath.
✦
Very slowly, Oscar raised one hand in a wave. He felt a little silly doing it.
But it felt right.

"Hello," he said. As quiet as he could.

The currawong tilted its head the other way. Then it opened its beak and sang — not a little chirp, but a big, rolling, warbling song that filled the whole forest. Like it had been waiting to tell someone something, and Oscar was finally close enough to hear.

"I think he likes you," Mum whispered from just behind his shoulder.

Oscar smiled so wide his face hurt.
✦
The currawong spread its wings and was gone — a swoop of black and white through the trees, its call fading until it was just an echo, and then nothing at all.

Oscar stood very still for a long moment, even though the bird was long gone.

All the way back to the car, he kept humming it — that wobbly, wonderful song. He hummed it at dinner. He hummed it in the bath. He hummed it one last time as he fell asleep.

His very own Noosa sound.
He'd never forget it.

Maya and the Ancient Mountains

A story set at the Glass House Mountains
⛰️
There are some mornings when the Glass House Mountains look like they belong to another world entirely.

Maya had seen them a hundred times from the car window — those ancient peaks rising suddenly from the flat green plains, each one a different shape, silent as old secrets. But today was different. Today she was going to walk right up to one.
✦
The track wound upward through scribbly gum and bottlebrush, the bark of the trees peeling away in long pale curls. Maya's legs ached in that good way — the way that meant she was working for something worth having.

She paused at a clearing and looked back. The valley spread below like a rumpled green quilt, and beyond it, the faint shimmer of the ocean. Caloundra's headland, the curving bays, the glint of Mooloolaba in the distance — all of it laid out like a map of everywhere she'd ever loved.
✦
"The Jinibara people called these mountains home for thousands of years," Dad said beside her. "Can you imagine growing up with this view every single day?"

Maya thought about that for a long while. She thought about what it would mean to know a place so well — every rock, every bird call, every shift of light across the valley — that it felt less like somewhere you visited and more like part of who you were.
✦
At the summit, the wind was sharp and clean, and Maya stood with her arms out and her face to the sky. She felt impossibly small and completely alive at the same time — which, she thought, might actually be the best feeling there is.

Below her, the mountains stretched in every direction: Beerwah, Tibrogargan, Coonowrin — each one watching over the Sunshine Coast in its own quiet, enduring way.

She would come back. She already knew it. Some places get into your bones like that.
